Studying the emergence and maintenance of biodiversity

"Simplifying" the conditions :
emergence and maintenance of diversity
in a long term evolution experiment

Laboratory experiments allow us to control for and simplify the complex conditions in which organisms evolve. This is invaluable when deciphering intricate systems and interactions. I was able to describe the emergence and maintenance of a long term coexistence in snowflake yeast. Two genotypes have evolved from an isogenic ancestor, in response to a trade off between growth and survival, and have coexisted for over 4,300 generations. See the full story here !

The joy of complexity :
mosaic of somatic mutations in one of Earth's largest organism,
a tale of space and time

Turning to the field, I pursued my desire to understand the rise of complexity, but this time from the mutational perspective. Clonally reproducing plants are also the longest living organisms. How is that possible, when every cell division is an opportunity from mutations (potentially deleterious) to arise, and be passed on to the progeny ? Quaking aspen can propagate vegetatively by sending roots from which new clonal stems grow. While a stem lifespand does exceed 150 years, clones are able to regenerate themselves from the root stock such that the organism as a whole can be older than its parts. To study somatic mutational dynamics and diversity in large and potentially long-lived organisms, I sampled leaves, roots and bark samples of the largest aspen clone known to date, the Pando clone. We followed the somatic mutations in space and time to descrbe the evolutionary history of the Pando clone. We uncover a weaker than expected spatial structure, and give a first estimate of the clone's age.

Adaptation :
how strong selective pressures shape plant genomes

Studying plant genomes and how they adapt to rapidly changing or harsh environments is crucial to understand the evolution of diversity and the changes we observe around us in both human-made and natural landscapes. To expand my understanding of plant adaptation and computanional as well as experimental skills, I have joined the Kreiner lab at the University of Chicago. My research focuses on the genetic architecture of drought adaptation, and its evolution in both time and space using herbarium and contemporary samples collected on a Southwest to Northeast gradient.
